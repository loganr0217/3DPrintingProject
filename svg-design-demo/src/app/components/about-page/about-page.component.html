Overview

Creating your custom lightscreen with LightScreen.art is a fun and easy process completed in just a few steps. In this overview, we'll outline the steps providing detail and background to help you make your absolute best lightscreen.

Step One

After clicking on the “Let's Start” button on our introductory page, we'll ask you a couple of quick questions about the window you plan to cover with your custom lightscreen. We need to know what type of window you plan to cover because lightscreens are constructed from multiple panels and we size those panels depending upon type to result in a lightscreen that completely covers the window glass.

First, we'll ask you about window dividers. In this case, windows come in three main types:

No Dividers: Those that are a single glass pane with nothing but glass between the sides of the window frame.

Raised Dividers: Those that have panes of glass that are physically separated by thin strips (usually wood). These windows are divided into multiple square or rectangular panes. Windows of this type are usually older or designed to look traditional. Until the 20th century making large plates of glass was difficult and expensive so most windows used dividers to make multiple small panes into a single large window.

Embedded Dividers: Those that have a single glass pane between the sides of the window frame but have dividers embedded between the two glass sheets in a double pane window. This type of window was a way of making a modern window look more traditional at a lesser cost than raised dividers. They were popular in the last half of the 20th century and less so today.

When there are no dividers, we size the panels based upon the window dimensions themselves and the dimensions of our 3D printers that build the lightscreen frame. Our algorithm is designed to cover your window with the fewest number of panels possible.

When there are raised dividers, we consider your window to be a number of relatively small panels, each covering the panes between the dividers. In this case, it's the dimensions of these panes, not the dimensions of the entire window that determine how we size panels to result in a complete lightscreen.

Finally, when there are embedded dividers we again consider the window to be a collection of panes but instead of having the lightscreen panels reside between the dividers, we size them such that the panels meet over the embedded dividers so that the two adjoining panel frames cover the embedded divider below. So whatever type of window you have, no dividers, raised dividers or embedded dividers, your lightscreen will both cover the glass and look great.

Pro Tip: Note that if your window has no dividers, we will select the number of panels required to cover your window, always going with the fewest number of panels possible. If you would prefer to set the panelization yourself, that may be achieved through a workaround: Although the window has no dividers, instead select the embedded divider option. Then select a number of dividers one less than your desired number of panels for that row or column, set the divider width to 0.0625 inches (1 mm) and then decrease the overall size of the window sides by the number of dividers on that side multiplied by the divider width. For example, if your window is 18 inches (457 mm) wide by 36 inches (914 mm) high, we will panelize it into one panel across and two panels down, each measuring 18 inches wide by 18 inches high. If you would prefer to have two panels across and four panels down, indicate one vertical divider and three horizontal dividers, 0.0625 inch (1 mm) divider width and decrease the overall window size to be 17.938 (456 mm) wide by 35.8125 inches (911 mm) high. Note that panels may be no smaller than 4 inches (100 mm) and no larger than 19 inches (480 mm) on any side.

After you select your window type by clicking one of the three window buttons, two types of windows will be displayed: Casement and Double Hung.

A casement window is a single window with a single window frame. Sometimes they don't open at all and in the cases where they do, it's usually done by turning a crank at the bottom of the window.

Double hung windows consist of two separate window frames that are opened by pulling the lower window (the lower sash) up to be in front of the upper sash. Double hung windows often have a two piece lock or locks on top of the lower sash frame and the bottom of the top sash frame. This lock complicates the design and installation of your lightscreen.

If your double hung window has a lock, you have a few options:

Remove the lock: If your window is in an area where you have no concerns about it being opened (from the inside or the outside) you can simply remove the lock. If you don't plan to open the window often, you can secure the window closed by means other than the lock such as screws or other fasteners.

Work around the lock: Our lightscreens are 3/8 inch (10 mm) thick. If the back portion of the lock on the bottom of the top sash frame is more than 3/8 inch from the window glass, the lightscreen will fit behind the lock.

Contact LightScreen.art: If neither of those options are viable, contact us at info@lightscreen.art and we'll do what we can to find a solution that works for you. We plan to enhance our web application to accommodate locks on double hung windows in the future.

When you click on either the Casement or the Double Hung window, you'll be advanced to the next step. Note that if you'd like to go back to a previous step, click the back button displayed on each step of our website and not the back button on your browser. Clicking the back button on your browser will return you not to an earlier step in the process but rather a previously visited web page.




Step Two

In this step, we'll ask you for details on the size of your window. First, we'll ask for your preferred unit of measure— we support both inches and millimeters. Note that currently we do not support fractional inches meaning that in the likely event that your measurements are not in whole inches, you'll need to enter decimal inches. For example, if your measurement is 12 3/8 inches, it must be entered as 12.375 inches. A conversion table may be found here. If you have no preference, millimeters is the best option as the internal workings of our site are based on that unit of measure. From there, the details we require are dependent upon your selections in the previous step. Specifically:

Casement windows with no dividers: We need only the width and height of the window glass from the inner side of one frame to the inner side of the opposite frame.

Casement windows with raised or embedded dividers: We need the width and height of the complete window glass, i.e. from one frame side of the other including the dividers. To work out the correct panelization for your lightscreen, we'll need a few other data points:

  • The number of horizontal dividers, i.e. the number of dividers that go from the left side of the window to the right side.

  • The number of vertical dividers, i.e. the number of dividers that go from the top of the window to the bottom.

  • The width of the dividers, i.e. the distance between one edge of the divider to the other edge. For raised dividers, measure at the bottom closest to the window glass.

With this information we'll calculate the size of the window panes between the dividers. For windows with raised dividers, the size of the calculated panes will be the size of the panels that collectively make up your lightscreen. For windows with embedded dividers the panels that make up the lightscreen will be larger by half the width of the divider on all sides where there is a divider so that the adjoining panel frames meet up over the embedded divider.

Double hung windows with no dividers: This is similar to measuring for casement windows with no dividers done twice. First, we'll ask you for the dimensions of the top sash, i.e. the window glass in the top frame. When complete, click the Switch to Bottom Sash button and provide the dimensions of the bottom sash, i.e. the window glass in the bottom frame. The dimensions for the two sashes are often identical but can differ for height and even width so measure them both individually.

Double hung windows with raised or embedded dividers: This is the most involved type of window to measure. We'll ask you for the width and height of the top sash, the number of horizontal and vertical dividers and the divider width. Then we'll repeat the same questions for the bottom sash after you've clicked the Switch to Bottom Sash button.

When you've entered all the window dimension information requested, click the next button. This will bring up a summary of your measurements for the overall window and, for windows with raised or embedded dividers, the calculated dimensions of the panes between the dividers. We urge you to double and even triple check all of the dimensions and correct them as necessary. If the calculated pane dimensions are inconsistent with your observed pane measurements, adjust the overall window measurement and/or the divider width until the dimensions in our system are consistent with your measured dimensions.

It cannot be stressed enough that providing us with accurate measurements is key to getting a lightscreen that you'll love. Lightscreen panels that are too small will let in noticeable rays of light, those that are too large will be difficult if not impossible to install. LightScreen.art will produce your panels to the exact sizes that correspond to the dimensions you provide and cannot be responsible for inaccurate measurements provided by our customers. Please (please) review our measurement tutorial for more information.




Step Three

In this step you'll be selecting a template for your custom lightscreen. These templates consist of a number of panels that are consistent with the measurements provided in the previous step. The templates are arranged by genre such as Artist Inspired, Garden, Interests and Classics. When you've clicked on a genre button all the templates available within that genre that match your window's dimensions will be displayed. We urge you to browse through all the genres to find the template you believe to be the best suited for the window you plan to cover. When you click on a template you'll be advanced to the next step.

Note that not all possible combinations of available panels are represented in templates. If you find that none of the available templates meet with your approval, it's possible to create custom templates from our complete set of available panels. To access LightScreen.art's Template Design Interface (TDI or “Teddy”) you must be a registered user. You may register by clicking the Signup button on the upper right of our website and following the prompts. Please review our TDI tutorial for more information.




Step Four

Having provided your window dimensions and selected a template, you're now ready to begin customizing your lightscreen by choosing colors for the individual panes from our extensive color palette. Our palette consists of two main types of finishes: smooth and frosted. The type of finish is indicated when you hover your mouse over a particular color swatch in our palette. Smooth finishes provide a glossy appearance and frosted finishes provide a more matte finish. You can mix and match smooth and frosted finishes on a single lightscreen or just go with one or the other. Regardless of finish, all our panes are translucent meaning that they'll let in light but not images: an important consideration when installing your lightscreen in places like a bath or entryway.

Pane color selection may be done on a manual, pane by pane basis, on an automatic basis or with a combination of the two. In any case, coloring in your lightscreen is more fun and easier than a coloring book or paint by numbers kit because we provide an undo button.

Manual Color Selection: After selecting your template by clicking on it, you'll be advanced to the coloring step. This section consists of three major areas: the active panel in the upper left, the color palette below the active panel and the complete template view on the right. Note that the template view is based upon default panel sizes and are not in the proportions associated with your specific window dimensions. The active panel may be changed to another panel by clicking on the desired panel in the complete template view. Only one panel, the active panel, may be colored at a time. To begin coloring the active panel, click on a swatch within the color palette. Then click on the panes in the active panel that you'd like to make that color. Selecting a different color and clicking on a previously colored pane will change the pane to the new color so you may experiment with multiple color selections. Note that as you move through this process, the pane colors will be reflected both in the active panel and in the template view.

Autofill Color Selection: For those who would like to expedite the color selection process and/or those who would like a bit of guidance from professional designers, LightScreen.art provides an autofill feature. Autofill is enabled by clicking the button in the upper left corner of the active panel area. When autofill is enabled, selecting a color from the palette and then clicking on a single pane will fill in not only that pane with that color but also every other pane on every other panel that our designers consider similar enough to be of the same color for the entire lightscreen. For example, if the chosen template depicts a rose, selecting red and then clicking on a single petal will make all other rose petals on all panels the same red. Note that autofill doesn't specify colors, rather it groups panes into various color groups so you may still customize your lightscreen to your desired color scheme. Using the same example, selecting yellow and then clicking on a single rose petal will color all other rose petals yellow.

Hybrid Color Selection: You may toggle between manual and autofill modes by clicking the autofill button on and off. It's typically better to start your design in autofill mode. Toggling modes will not erase previously selected colors whether they were applied using the manual mode or the autofill mode. However, when autofill mode is on and a single previously colored pane is changed to a new color, all other panes that are considered to be similar by our designers will change to the new color: click the undo button if you make a mistake. Using this hybrid approach, you can gain the benefits of both modes: the speed and guidance of autofill and the granular detail of manual mode. Continuing with the rose example above, you may start with autofill to color all rose petals red and all rose stems green. Then you may toggle autofill off and color manually selected rose petals yellow on a pane by pane basis.

Continue this process or processes until all the panes in all the panels are of your desired colors then click Next Step to advance to a summary of your order.
